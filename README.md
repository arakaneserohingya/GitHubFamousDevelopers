# Android Personal Technical Open-Source Influence Ranking (Irregularly Updated)

* Project Address: [Github](https://github.com/getActivity/AndroidGithubBoss)

* One day, I had a sudden idea that there are many Android gurus involved in open-source projects, but most people are unaware of who they are. So, I decided to open-source a project to bring together these Android open-source gurus. Not only can people get to know these gurus, but they can also discover their outstanding open-source works, which can be a valuable source of learning and reference for us.

* As a result, I personally collected the names of these individuals. All the data presented here has been manually collected, and the entire process took nearly half a year. It's provided for your reference. If there are any omissions, please notify me through [issue](https://github.com/getActivity/AndroidGithubBoss/issues/new) to include them. If there are any errors, kindly inform me through [issue](https://github.com/getActivity/AndroidGithubBoss/issues/new) for corrections.

#### Standards and Requirements:


* Listing Rules:

  * Must not violate the spirit of open-source.
  * Must be an Android developer.
  * Must open-source projects under your own name.
  * Stars count must not be artificially inflated by any means.
  * Only individual accounts with 3,000 or more Stars will be included.

* Ranking Rules:

  * Ranking is based on the effective number of Stars from high to low.
  * If an individual's Stars count is less than 10,000, the effective Stars count is directly based on the total number of their Stars.
  * If an individual's Stars count is greater than 10,000 but less than 20,000, the effective Stars count must account for **60%** of their total Stars count to be considered in the ranking calculation. Otherwise, it will be based solely on the effective Stars count.
  * If an individual's Stars count is greater than 20,000 but less than 30,000, the effective Stars count must account for **70%** of their total Stars count to be considered in the ranking calculation. Otherwise, it will be based solely on the effective Stars count.
  * If an individual's Stars count is greater than 30,000 but less than 40,000, the effective Stars count must account for **80%** of their total Stars count to be considered in the ranking calculation. Otherwise, it will be based solely on the effective Stars count.
  * If an individual's Stars count is greater than 40,000 or more, the effective Stars count must account for **90%** of their total Stars count to be considered in the ranking calculation. Otherwise, it will be based solely on the effective Stars count.

* Definition of Effective Stars Count:

  * Must be related to open-source projects relevant to Android, regardless of the programming language or project type.
  * Exclude Stars from open-source projects not targeting the Android platform, such as Flutter, ReactNative, Weex, and others.
  * Exclude Stars from open-source projects unrelated to Android, such as algorithm problems, resume templates, emojis, and others.
  * Exclude Stars obtained from simple forks of other open-source projects (without any original contributions).

* Rules for Recommending Open-Source Projects:

  * Projects must not be simple demo projects.
  * A maximum of three open-source projects can be recommended.
  * Projects must be related to Android development.
  * Exclude projects related to cross-platform development like Flutter and React Native.

* Rules for Marking Open-Source Projects as Outdated:

  * Based on Eclipse projects.
  * Clearly stated that the project is no longer maintained.
  * The repository of the open-source project has been marked as read-only.
  * Code has not been updated for three months, and no issues have been addressed in over three months.
  * Commits that only modify the `README.md` file are not considered updates.
  * Non-timely updates; projects are checked for updates once a year, focusing on those not marked as outdated.
  * Once an open-source project is marked as outdated, it cannot be deleted, and the author needs to address the issue to have it removed.

* Criteria for Identifying Star Manipulation of Open-Source Projects:

  * Analyze the Star Trend Chart at [Star Chart](https://starchart.cc/) to identify sharp angles close to 90 degrees, indicating potential Star manipulation.
  * Examine the number of issues, the author's response speed, and issue resolution to assess the project's maintenance quality.
  * Review the commit history, including the quantity of code, code quality, and commit descriptions, to evaluate the project's development activity.
  * Compare the number of forks with the number of Stars, which should generally have a ratio between `10:1` and `10:2`.
  * For frameworks published on [JitPack repository](https://jitpack.io/), check monthly download counts, which should ideally exceed the current number of Stars, typically ranging from 2 to 10 times the number of Stars.

#### Why are so many open-source projects not maintained?

* This is a question many people ask, as they often can't understand why good open-source projects are left unmaintained. Several main reasons contribute to this situation:

  * No financial gain: Open-source projects being abandoned is a perfectly normal occurrence. One harsh reality is that most open-source projects, even if widely used and praised by many, do not generate any financial benefits for their authors. In other words, creating and maintaining these projects is essentially a labor of love. Unfortunately, when there's no income generated, it means running the project is actually a loss-making endeavor since there are still living expenses to cover, such as rent, utilities, and food. As much as people support open-source authors by giving them Stars and follows, these gestures don't provide any financial gains to support their livelihood.

  * Low status: A common saying goes, "Teachers are like candles, burning themselves to light others." Similarly, open-source authors embody this spirit, voluntarily contributing their time and effort without any financial compensation. However, unlike teachers who receive respect and have a recognized social status, open-source authors often find themselves lacking respect and acknowledgment within the industry. They may face situations like:

    1. People approaching them for help without showing gratitude once the issue is resolved since open-source authors are expected to serve the community for free.
    2. Individuals seeking help with problems unrelated to the framework, even though it falls outside the author's responsibility.
    3. Copying the author's code to their projects and removing the author's attribution from the header comments, replacing it with their own name.
    4. Maintaining a separate version of the framework locally and seeking assistance from the author when they encounter difficulties.
    5. Repeatedly asking for help without reading the documentation or taking the time to find a solution on their own.
    6. Sending friend requests to authors under the pretense of discussing technical matters but immediately asking for help with their personal technical problems.
    7. Blaming and sometimes even verbally abusing authors in public forums when facing issues, with little to no one stepping in to intervene.
    8. Some people even take a well-established framework, make minor changes to the public API names, and release it as their own work, promoting it as an original creation.

    ...and more.

  * Change in nature: Do you still remember the original intention of open-source? One person opensource a project, and many people come together to improve it. However, the current reality often sees users of open-source projects immediately turning to the author when they encounter problems, submitting issues and expecting the author to fix everything. If the author decides to abandon the project, they might face criticism. Some may accuse the author of being irresponsible and ungrateful for the support they've received. However, the truth is that open-source authors have no obligation to address issues or provide support, as they haven't been financially compensated for their efforts. Imagine this from another perspective: if you earned money using an open-source project, would you share your profits with the author? It's a natural consequence that open-source authors might choose not to maintain a project in this situation. In reality, people are selfish, and one shouldn't claim to be a saint.

Overall, the challenges and lack of support, coupled with the absence of financial rewards, contribute to the abandonment of many open-source projects.

* Regarding this, I would like to share my feelings and thoughts about open-source:

   * Open-source authors are great: Let me ask you a question, imagine a company hiring you, but the boss says you won't be paid and you have to keep working indefinitely. How long would you be able to endure this situation? Open-source operates on a similar model - authors continuously contribute without making any money. Many people might find this unacceptable and reject such an offer outright. However, there are still some open-source authors who persevere for a long time before giving up maintenance. This is undoubtedly an act of great dedication. Put yourself in their shoes - would you do the same? We should understand that being open-source authors usually occupies their spare time. While others relax on weekends, open-source authors work on adding features and fixing bugs for their projects. They could choose to relax and play games on Saturdays and Sundays, but they opt for a path that few dare to take, demonstrating great commitment. So, there's no such thing as an idyllic life; it's just someone else carrying the burden for you. Additionally, whether open-source authors continue to maintain their projects or not, they are still great. Their past contributions should not be nullified simply because they have stopped maintaining the projects. We should show them respect and recognition.

   * Please treat open-source authors kindly: As an open-source author, I have experienced many things I shouldn't have. For instance, many people come to me with various technical difficulties. When I had few followers, I tried to help solve their problems, but as my following grew, I realized that I couldn't handle the overwhelming amount of inquiries. Let me ask you, can you withstand dozens of people asking you technical questions every day? I could choose not to reply, but I politely provide a standardized response stating that I currently don't handle issues outside the scope of the framework. This experience made me feel that once you become an open-source author, you're inviting a lot of trouble. People come to me with all sorts of issues, even those unrelated to the framework. It's not that I don't want to help, but like everyone else, my work and open-source commitments have already drained me, leaving little time and energy for anything else. I find myself overburdened and struggling to cope.

   * Don't act like a technical "big baby": I also feel that many people on the internet are frivolous and frequently bother others. In real life, this would accumulate a debt of gratitude, and if you don't show any appreciation after receiving help, people would ignore you the next time you need assistance. However, on the internet, people don't seem to have any concerns about this. Some completely disregard the feelings of open-source authors and believe they are entitled to their help because it's easy for the "big shot" author to assist them. Some don't even bother saying thank you after receiving help. To this, I can only say: When others help you, it's a favor; when they don't, it's not their obligation.

   * No open-source project is maintained indefinitely: Many people see that numerous open-source projects are not being maintained, and they think the Android ecosystem is in trouble. But this is a normal phenomenon, and if you look at the iOS industry, it's pretty much the same. I want to tell you that no open-source project is maintained indefinitely because open-source projects don't make money, and the authors still need to make a living. When you're older and have a family to support, do you think it's realistic to maintain an open-source project without any income? So, maintenance is a favor, and not maintaining is just a matter of course. I am currently maintaining open-source projects, mainly because I am not married yet, and I have a job to support myself. However, if one day I leave the Android industry for some reason, there's a high probability that I will stop maintaining my open-source projects. No open-source author can maintain their projects forever; there will come a day when they stop. We should cherish their contributions while they are active.

* In the general understanding, it may seem abnormal for open-source projects to be abandoned. However, after reading the above statements, you might come to believe that it is, in fact, a very normal occurrence. Nevertheless, someone has to carry the flag of open-source, and this generation of open-source contributors will eventually reach a point where they can no longer carry it. The responsibility of carrying this flag will then be passed on to the younger generation in the industry. Whether there will be successors willing to take on this responsibility depends on how the community's attitude towards open-source evolves in the future. If the attitude remains as it is now, it might be quite difficult to find successors. As for how to bring about change, it will require joint efforts from everyone.
